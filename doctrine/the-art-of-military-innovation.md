## Practice 1: Build essential forces during active operations

**Problem**  
Waiting for ideal conditions leaves urgent defense needs unmet.

**Action**  
Create simple units and improve them through immediate combat experience.

**Outcome**  
The army gains usable strength while building better methods.

Chapter: Raising an Army under Fire

## Practice 2: Turn shortages into focused experiments

**Problem**  
Limited resources prevent the use of standard solutions.

**Action**  
Test affordable ways to meet the most urgent operational need.

**Outcome**  
Scarcity produces simpler solutions that can be fielded quickly.

Chapter: How Scarcity Can Force Innovation

## Practice 3: Give young officers real responsibility

**Problem**  
Long promotion paths can delay new ideas and decisive action.

**Action**  
Place capable young officers in command roles early.

**Outcome**  
Fresh experience reaches important decisions faster.

Chapter: A Youthful Officer Corps

## Practice 4: Let field units solve local problems

**Problem**  
Central leaders often lack the details needed to fix battlefield problems.

**Action**  
Allow field units to test solutions and report what works.

**Outcome**  
Useful changes spread from direct operational experience.

Chapter: Innovation from Below

## Practice 5: Use civilian expertise in reserve units

**Problem**  
A closed military can miss useful knowledge from civilian work.

**Action**  
Assign reservists where their civilian skills can solve military problems.

**Outcome**  
The armed forces gain current technical and professional knowledge.

Chapter: A Reserve Army of Innovators

## Practice 6: Connect developers directly with military users

**Problem**  
The distance between industry and soldiers can produce equipment that misses real needs.

**Action**  
Let engineers observe operations and work directly with users.

**Outcome**  
New equipment fits battlefield conditions more closely.

Chapter: A Different Military-Industrial Complex

## Practice 7: Develop critical systems in short cycles

**Problem**  
Slow development can make a solution arrive after the threat has changed.

**Action**  
Build, test, and improve critical systems through short development cycles.

**Outcome**  
Effective technology reaches operational units sooner.

Chapter: High-Speed Development: From Missile Boats to Iron Dome

## Practice 8: Use the full talent of military women

**Problem**  
Limits on women's roles waste skills that the force needs.

**Action**  
Give qualified women access to technical work and operational responsibility.

**Outcome**  
A broader talent pool produces more useful ideas.

Chapter: IDF Women as Innovators

## Practice 9: Keep doctrine open to battlefield evidence

**Problem**  
Rigid doctrine can preserve methods that no longer match the threat.

**Action**  
Revise doctrine when exercises and operations reveal a better approach.

**Outcome**  
Military methods remain useful as conditions change.

Chapter: Military Doctrine and Innovation

## Practice 10: Challenge the assumptions behind past victories

**Problem**  
A major victory can make leaders trust methods that an enemy has learned to defeat.

**Action**  
Test successful methods against new enemy capabilities before the next war.

**Outcome**  
The force finds dangerous weaknesses before combat exposes them.

Chapter: From Triumph to Failure in the Air, 1967 and 1973

## Practice 11: Combine new technology with new tactics

**Problem**  
Advanced equipment has limited value when old tactics control its use.

**Action**  
Redesign tactics and training around the strengths of new technology.

**Outcome**  
Technological gains become reliable operational power.

Chapter: Airpower Restored with a Technological Leap

## Practice 12: Reproduce excellence through demanding training

**Problem**  
Elite performance remains rare when it depends only on exceptional individuals.

**Action**  
Use strict selection and repeated realistic training to build elite units.

**Outcome**  
High performance becomes a repeatable result.

Chapter: Elite Units: The Mass Production of Military Excellence

## Practice 13: Back officers who pursue urgent ideas

**Problem**  
Unusual operational needs often receive little support from normal military channels.

**Action**  
Give persistent officers room to build and prove specialized capabilities.

**Outcome**  
Promising ideas can become effective special forces.

Chapter: Military Entrepreneurs and Special Forces

## Practice 14: Combine firm discipline with local improvisation

**Problem**  
Strict control alone cannot solve every problem faced by armored units.

**Action**  
Enforce common standards while allowing crews to adapt equipment in the field.

**Outcome**  
Armored units stay coordinated and respond quickly to practical problems.

Chapter: The Armored Corps: Discipline and Technological Improvisation

## Practice 15: Design equipment around local combat needs

**Problem**  
Imported designs may protect priorities that do not fit local operations.

**Action**  
Build vehicles around the terrain and crew protection needs of the expected battlefield.

**Outcome**  
The equipment serves the force's actual mission more effectively.

Chapter: Why the Merkava Is Different

## Practice 16: Pair intelligence work with rapid engineering

**Problem**  
Intelligence loses value when units cannot quickly turn it into usable tools.

**Action**  
Place analysts and engineers together to solve urgent operational problems.

**Outcome**  
New intelligence capabilities reach users while they still matter.

Chapter: Units 8200 and 81
