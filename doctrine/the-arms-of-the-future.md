## Practice 1: Hide forces from constant observation

**Problem**  
Persistent sensors can expose visible or electronic activity before a force can act.

**Action**  
Reduce signatures and use concealment, deception, and frequent movement.

**Outcome**  
Forces survive longer and retain the chance to surprise the enemy.

Chapter: From mechanized to informatized warfare - Navigating the transparent battlefield

## Practice 2: Build communications that survive interference

**Problem**  
Dependence on a few radio and data links allows electronic attacks to disrupt the entire force.

**Action**  
Use dispersed networks that can change frequencies and continue operating with limited contact.

**Outcome**  
Units can coordinate even when the enemy contests the spectrum.

Chapter: From mechanized to informatized warfare - Contesting the spectrum

## Practice 3: Combine protection with mobility and deception

**Problem**  
Modern weapons can defeat heavy armor once sensors locate its position.

**Action**  
Protect forces through rapid movement, dispersed positions, hidden signatures, and layered defenses.

**Outcome**  
The enemy has fewer clear opportunities to deliver effective attacks.

Chapter: From mechanized to informatized warfare - When protection is an illusion

## Practice 4: Defend the support network

**Problem**  
Attacks on logistics can stop combat units without directly defeating them.

**Action**  
Disperse supplies and give support units the means to detect, avoid, and resist attacks.

**Outcome**  
Combat power remains available during sustained operations.

Chapter: From mechanized to informatized warfare - When the tail needs teeth

## Practice 5: Prepare combined teams for urban combat

**Problem**  
Buildings and civilians restrict observation, movement, and firepower in cities.

**Action**  
Train small teams to combine infantry, engineers, sensors, armor, and precise fires.

**Outcome**  
Units can clear urban ground with fewer casualties and less damage.

Chapter: From mechanized to informatized warfare - Blood in the streets

## Practice 6: Disperse forces and concentrate effects

**Problem**  
Concentrated formations are easy targets for modern sensors and long-range weapons.

**Action**  
Place units across a wide area and connect them so they can act together at decisive moments.

**Outcome**  
The force gains striking power without presenting one large target.

Chapter: The arms of the future - The geometry of the future battlefield

## Practice 7: Organize movement around shared information

**Problem**  
Independent units lose momentum when they cannot see threats or coordinate routes.

**Action**  
Link sensors, commanders, and mobile units through a resilient common picture.

**Outcome**  
Units can move through gaps and exploit opportunities faster.

Chapter: The arms of the future - The manoeuvre system

## Practice 8: Connect every sensor to suitable firepower

**Problem**  
Targets escape when information moves slowly between observers and weapons.

**Action**  
Create rapid links that match detected targets with available guns, missiles, or aircraft.

**Outcome**  
Fires arrive sooner and produce a greater effect with fewer shots.

Chapter: The arms of the future - The fires system

## Practice 9: Suppress defenses before closing with them

**Problem**  
Assault troops suffer heavy losses when enemy sensors and weapons remain active.

**Action**  
Use fires, electronic attack, smoke, engineers, and machines to open a protected route.

**Outcome**  
Assault forces reach and seize positions with lower risk.

Chapter: The arms of the future - The assault system

## Practice 10: Make support mobile and resilient

**Problem**  
Static and centralized support systems cannot sustain dispersed forces under attack.

**Action**  
Use smaller mobile supply, repair, medical, and engineering elements close to operating units.

**Outcome**  
Dispersed units recover and continue fighting for longer.

Chapter: The arms of the future - The support system

## Practice 11: Coordinate domains toward one political goal

**Problem**  
Military domains can pursue separate advantages that do not produce a useful strategic result.

**Action**  
Set one political purpose and assign each domain a clear supporting role.

**Outcome**  
Military activity creates effects that reinforce the intended policy.

Chapter: The continuation of policy - Divergent domains

## Practice 12: Transform around urgent operational problems

**Problem**  
Transformation fails when institutions buy new equipment without changing force design and training.

**Action**  
Set priorities from likely battlefield problems and test complete solutions in realistic exercises.

**Outcome**  
New technology becomes a usable combat capability instead of isolated equipment.

Chapter: The continuation of policy - Priorities in transformation

## Practice 13: Design forces for political usefulness

**Problem**  
A powerful force has limited value if leaders cannot use it for likely policy goals.

**Action**  
Build adaptable forces that can deploy, endure, escalate, and cooperate with partners.

**Outcome**  
Military power gives national leaders credible choices across different crises.

Chapter: The continuation of policy - An instrument of power
