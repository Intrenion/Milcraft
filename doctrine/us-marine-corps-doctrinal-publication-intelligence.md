## Practice 1: Treat intelligence as essential to every decision

**Problem**  
Commanders who act without intelligence risk misunderstanding the enemy and the environment.

**Action**  
Use intelligence before planning, deciding, and acting.

**Outcome**  
Decisions become better informed and less vulnerable to surprise.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - How Important is Intelligence?

## Practice 2: Reduce uncertainty about the enemy

**Problem**  
Uncertainty prevents commanders from choosing effective actions.

**Action**  
Collect and analyze information that answers the commander's most important questions.

**Outcome**  
The commander can act with greater confidence.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - The Objectives of Intelligence

## Practice 3: Build useful knowledge from information

**Problem**  
Raw information does not explain what the enemy can or may do.

**Action**  
Organize and interpret relevant information into knowledge for decisions.

**Outcome**  
The commander receives a clearer picture of the situation.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - Intelligence as Knowledge

## Practice 4: Maintain intelligence as a continuous process

**Problem**  
A changing situation quickly makes old intelligence unreliable.

**Action**  
Direct, collect, process, analyze, share, and update intelligence continuously.

**Outcome**  
Intelligence stays relevant as conditions change.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - Intelligence as a Process

## Practice 5: Make intelligence understandable and accessible

**Problem**  
Secrecy and specialized methods can make intelligence seem mysterious to its users.

**Action**  
Explain intelligence judgments and methods in clear language while protecting sensitive details.

**Outcome**  
Users understand the intelligence and apply it correctly.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - Why the Mystery?

## Practice 6: Judge meaning under uncertain conditions

**Problem**  
Intelligence often depends on incomplete information about a thinking enemy.

**Action**  
Combine evidence with reasoned judgment and state the remaining uncertainty.

**Outcome**  
Commanders receive useful assessments without false certainty.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - What Makes Intelligence Different?

## Practice 7: Set realistic expectations for intelligence

**Problem**  
Demanding certainty can hide uncertainty and distort intelligence judgments.

**Action**  
Expect timely estimates that identify assumptions, gaps, and confidence levels.

**Outcome**  
Commanders can weigh intelligence within its real limits.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - Expectations of Intelligence

## Practice 8: Turn abundant information into relevant intelligence

**Problem**  
Large amounts of information can overwhelm analysts and distract commanders.

**Action**  
Filter information according to priority intelligence needs.

**Outcome**  
Important facts reach decision makers before they lose value.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - Intelligence in the Information Age

## Practice 9: Combine technology with sound analysis

**Problem**  
Advanced collection systems cannot by themselves explain enemy plans or battlefield conditions.

**Action**  
Integrate technical collection with human judgment and operational context.

**Outcome**  
Commanders gain a more complete view of the enemy.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - A Case Study: Desert Storm 1990-1991

## Practice 10: Use intelligence to support action

**Problem**  
Intelligence has little value when it does not help a commander decide or act.

**Action**  
Connect every intelligence effort to a practical command need.

**Outcome**  
Intelligence directly improves operational effectiveness.

Chapter: The Nature of Intelligence - Conclusion

## Practice 11: Study the enemy as an adaptive system

**Problem**  
Knowing isolated enemy facts does not reveal how the enemy may fight.

**Action**  
Assess the enemy's organization, capabilities, methods, goals, and likely reactions.

**Outcome**  
Commanders can anticipate a wider range of enemy actions.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - What Do We Want to Know About the Enemy?

## Practice 12: Deliver timely, relevant, and accurate intelligence

**Problem**  
Intelligence that arrives late or misses the commander's need cannot guide action.

**Action**  
Prioritize relevance, timeliness, accuracy, usability, and completeness in every product.

**Outcome**  
Commanders receive intelligence they can use when decisions are required.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Characteristics of Good Intelligence

## Practice 13: Match intelligence to the decision

**Problem**  
Different decisions require different types of intelligence.

**Action**  
Select the intelligence class that fits the mission and planning need.

**Outcome**  
Each decision receives information at the right scope and level of detail.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Classes of Intelligence

## Practice 14: Separate enemy capability from enemy intention

**Problem**  
An enemy may possess a capability without intending to use it.

**Action**  
Assess what the enemy can do separately from what the enemy is likely to do.

**Outcome**  
Commanders avoid treating every possible action as equally probable.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Capabilities Versus Intentions

## Practice 15: Separate meaningful indicators from background activity

**Problem**  
Routine events and deceptive actions can hide signs of enemy activity.

**Action**  
Compare new information with expected patterns and test competing explanations.

**Outcome**  
Analysts detect important changes with fewer false warnings.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Signals and Noise

## Practice 16: Align intelligence with the level of command

**Problem**  
Intelligence at the wrong scale can confuse planning and waste resources.

**Action**  
Provide strategic, operational, or tactical intelligence according to the commander's responsibility.

**Outcome**  
Commanders receive the scope of intelligence needed for their decisions.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Levels of Intelligence

## Practice 17: Turn command needs into clear intelligence requirements

**Problem**  
Vague questions produce scattered collection and unfocused analysis.

**Action**  
State each intelligence requirement as a specific question tied to a decision.

**Outcome**  
Collection and analysis focus on information that matters most.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Intelligence Requirements

## Practice 18: Use multiple independent intelligence sources

**Problem**  
A single source may be incomplete, mistaken, biased, or deceptive.

**Action**  
Compare information from human, technical, documentary, and open sources.

**Outcome**  
Confirmed findings become more reliable and gaps become easier to see.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Sources of Intelligence

## Practice 19: Integrate all intelligence functions

**Problem**  
Disconnected collection, analysis, production, and sharing create gaps in understanding.

**Action**  
Coordinate intelligence functions around common requirements and operational needs.

**Outcome**  
The intelligence effort produces a coherent picture for the commander.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Functions of Intelligence

## Practice 20: Protect intelligence without blocking its use

**Problem**  
Weak security exposes operations while excessive control delays needed information.

**Action**  
Limit access according to risk while sharing intelligence with authorized users promptly.

**Outcome**  
Sensitive information remains protected and useful.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Security

## Practice 21: Keep the intelligence cycle responsive

**Problem**  
A rigid sequence can delay intelligence in a fast changing situation.

**Action**  
Run direction, collection, processing, analysis, sharing, and feedback as overlapping activities.

**Outcome**  
New intelligence reaches users quickly and guides further effort.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - The Intelligence Cycle

## Practice 22: Reassess the enemy when evidence changes

**Problem**  
Fixed assumptions can cause analysts to miss a major enemy action.

**Action**  
Challenge current estimates whenever new indicators conflict with them.

**Outcome**  
Intelligence adapts before outdated beliefs cause operational surprise.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - A Case Study: Vietnam 1972

## Practice 23: Apply intelligence theory with judgment

**Problem**  
Rules and models cannot remove uncertainty from intelligence work.

**Action**  
Use doctrine as a guide while adapting analysis to the mission and situation.

**Outcome**  
Intelligence remains disciplined without becoming rigid.

Chapter: Intelligence Theory - Conclusion

## Practice 24: Prepare for uncertainty and enemy adaptation

**Problem**  
Incomplete information and active deception prevent a perfect view of the enemy.

**Action**  
Develop flexible estimates that include plausible enemy actions and key uncertainties.

**Outcome**  
The command can respond when events differ from the main forecast.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - The Challenge to Intelligence

## Practice 25: Make the commander responsible for intelligence

**Problem**  
Intelligence loses focus when commanders leave it entirely to specialists.

**Action**  
Commanders must state their needs, guide priorities, and evaluate the results.

**Outcome**  
The intelligence effort stays tied to command decisions.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Intelligence Is a Command Responsibility

## Practice 26: Maintain direct contact between commanders and intelligence staff

**Problem**  
Indirect communication can distort the commander's questions and the analyst's judgments.

**Action**  
Create frequent direct exchanges about decisions, assumptions, findings, and gaps.

**Outcome**  
Intelligence answers the commander's actual needs more quickly.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - The Command-Intelligence Connection

## Practice 27: Integrate intelligence with operations

**Problem**  
Separate intelligence and operations staffs can develop conflicting views of the mission.

**Action**  
Plan intelligence collection and operational actions together from the start.

**Outcome**  
Operations use current intelligence and create new opportunities to learn.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - The Intelligence-Operations Connection

## Practice 28: Build intelligence through shared effort

**Problem**  
No single person or unit can collect and understand all relevant information.

**Action**  
Require every element to report observations and contribute its expertise.

**Outcome**  
The command develops a broader and more accurate picture.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Intelligence as a Team Effort

## Practice 29: Deliver finished judgments instead of raw data

**Problem**  
Providing access to information does not ensure that users understand its meaning.

**Action**  
Analyze available information and present a clear assessment for the decision.

**Outcome**  
Commanders spend less time interpreting data and more time acting.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Intelligence Is a Product, Not a Provision

## Practice 30: Balance collection, analysis, and sharing

**Problem**  
Overinvesting in one intelligence activity weakens the entire effort.

**Action**  
Allocate people, time, and tools across collection, analysis, production, and distribution.

**Outcome**  
The intelligence system supports decisions without a critical weak point.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - A Balanced Approach

## Practice 31: Concentrate intelligence on priority decisions

**Problem**  
Trying to answer every question spreads limited resources too thinly.

**Action**  
Rank intelligence requirements by their importance to upcoming decisions.

**Outcome**  
Critical questions receive attention before less important requests.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Focusing the Intelligence Effort

## Practice 32: Use intelligence to increase operational speed

**Problem**  
Slow intelligence forces commanders to pause or act with outdated knowledge.

**Action**  
Anticipate decision points and deliver assessments before the commander needs them.

**Outcome**  
The command can act faster than the enemy can respond.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Generating Tempo Through Intelligence

## Practice 33: Train intelligence and operations personnel together

**Problem**  
Separate training prevents teams from understanding each other's needs and methods.

**Action**  
Practice realistic intelligence tasks within command and operational exercises.

**Outcome**  
The team coordinates more effectively during actual operations.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Intelligence Education and Training

## Practice 34: Understand local conditions before acting

**Problem**  
Weak knowledge of people, groups, terrain, and intentions can turn a limited mission into a crisis.

**Action**  
Build detailed local intelligence and update it through continuous contact and observation.

**Outcome**  
Forces can identify threats and adapt their actions to the environment.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - A Case Study: Somalia 1992-1993

## Practice 35: Build intelligence into command from the beginning

**Problem**  
Intelligence added late cannot fully shape plans, preparations, or decisions.

**Action**  
Integrate commanders, intelligence staff, operators, and collectors throughout the mission.

**Outcome**  
The command gains shared understanding and acts with greater unity.

Chapter: Creating Effective Intelligence - Conclusion
